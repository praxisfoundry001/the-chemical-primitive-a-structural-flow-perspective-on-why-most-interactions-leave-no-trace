# The Chemical Primitive: A Structural Flow Perspective on Why Most Interactions Leave No Trace

## What is this?
A foundational bridge artifact in the chemistry lane of the Structural Flow library.

This paper asks a simple structural question:
why do some chemical interactions matter while most leave no lasting trace?

Its answer is austere:
under repeated interaction, erasure is normal and persistence is expensive.

## Why should I care?
This artifact gives chemistry a cleaner structural starting point.

Instead of beginning with substances, mechanisms, or reaction success stories, it begins with repeated interaction under entropy and asks what must happen for any interaction to acquire durability, forward authority, and local time attachment at all.

## What is the claim?
The paper argues that:
- chemistry begins in a bath that permits repeated interaction without guaranteeing persistence
- entropy is best read here as memory erasure under repeated interaction
- boundaries emerge as the minimal structural response to erasure
- most chemically relevant persistence attempts fail
- those failures can be described locally through a chemistry-facing descriptor set without replacing the already-closed cross-domain failure architecture of Structural Flow

## What is this not?
This artifact is not:
- a catalog of substances or reactions
- a mechanism-level chemistry theory
- a new primitive beyond current Structural Flow canon
- a replacement for the already-closed cross-domain failure map

It is a chemistry-lane foundation artifact that identifies the structural starting condition and local failure grammar of chemical persistence.

## How was this done?
The paper proceeds in order:
1. chemistry under repeated interaction
2. entropy as memory erasure
3. the chemical bath
4. boundary formation as the first ratchet
5. chemistry-local failure expressions of non-participation
6. implications for persistence, cascade, and complexity
